# Climate Funds Accreditation & Disbursement: GCF, GEF & CIF

**Research Assistant work, SOAS Centre for Sustainable Finance** — under Prof. Ulrich Volz and Dr. Yuen Lo
**Author:** David Ayuuya Ayeliga | 2026

## Overview

This project examines why the accreditation and disbursement pathways of the three largest multilateral climate funds — the **Green Climate Fund (GCF)**, the **Global Environment Facility (GEF)**, and the **Climate Investment Funds (CIF)** — remain slow and difficult for developing-country institutions to navigate, even as each fund pursues its own reform agenda. The work combines a full research report on the GCF with comparative research briefs on the GEF and CIF, building toward a cross-fund view of where climate finance pipelines stick and what reforms are underway to fix them.

## Key Findings

- **GCF** disbursement lags approval badly (a 31% disbursement-to-approval ratio), driven by a five-step accreditation pathway that is especially burdensome for national Direct Access Entities. Recommended reforms include standardising Funded Activity Agreements to remove the gap between Board approval and legal signing, extending the screening response window for LDC/SIDS-based DFIs, and expanding project-first accreditation routes. A practical near-term fix for DFIs: build a track record via GEF or Adaptation Fund accreditation first, since it is faster and one completed project unlocks GCF credibility — a route already used successfully by Rwanda, Senegal, and Bangladesh.
- **GEF** performs much better on disbursement (76% disbursement-to-approval, against the GCF's 31%) but accreditation costs remain prohibitive for most national institutions, and timelines have deteriorated since GEF-7 — concentrating resources in a handful of large multilaterals rather than the national agencies closest to project communities. GEF-9 introduces the facility's most substantive reform package to date.
- **CIF** sidesteps agency-level accreditation entirely — its six permanent MDB delivery partners are pre-approved — so its bottleneck sits instead in the Investment Plan pipeline itself: co-financing commitments must be locked in 2–3 years before implementation, and grants for MDB-delivered projects cannot disburse until that bank's own sovereign loan is signed. CIF's admin costs (2.6% of cumulative funding decisions) and MDB implementation fees (0.7–3.9%) undercut both the GCF and GEF by a wide margin, and in January 2025 it became the first multilateral climate fund to raise capital directly from bond markets (the CIF Capital Markets Mechanism).
- Read together, the three funds show a common pattern: **the more a fund gates access through upfront institutional accreditation, the slower disbursement tends to be** — while funds that pre-approve delivery partners (like CIF's MDB model) move faster but concentrate delivery in large multilaterals rather than national institutions.

## Methods & Frameworks

Comparative institutional analysis of accreditation/disbursement architecture across three multilateral climate funds, using each fund's own portfolio dashboards, board documents, and independent evaluation office reports (GCF Revised Accreditation Framework B.42/13; GCF IEU SAP Evaluation; AADFI/WFDFI DFI mapping study; GEF and CIF programme performance data).
